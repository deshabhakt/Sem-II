Quadrature formulas¶

Numerical integration or quadrature formulae have the form:

∫baf(x)dx≈ω0f(x0)+ω1f(x1)+⋯+ωnf(xn)∫abf(x)dx≈ω0f(x0)+ω1f(x1)+⋯+ωnf(xn)

where x0,x1,...,xnx0,x1,...,xn (nodes) are n+1n+1 different points in the interval [a,b][a,b] and ω0,ω1,…,ωnω0,ω1,…,ωn (weights) are real numbers.

Interpolatory quadrature formulas¶

If PnPn is the polynomial interpolation function for ff in the nodes x0,x1,...,xn∈[a,b]x0,x1,...,xn∈[a,b] and

∫baf(x)dx≈∫baPn(x)dx=ω0f(x0)+ω1f(x1)+⋯+ωnf(xn)∫abf(x)dx≈∫abPn(x)dx=ω0f(x0)+ω1f(x1)+⋯+ωnf(xn)

this is called an interpolatory quadrature formula.

Simple and composite quadrature formulas¶

Quadrature formulas are called simple if the approximation takes place in the whole interval (a,b)(a,b), and composite if, before the application of the formula, we split the interval (a,b)(a,b) in a given number, nn, of subintervals.

Degree of precision¶

The degree of precision is used for simple quadrature formulas. A quadrature formula has degree of precision rr if the rule is exact for

f(x)=1,f(x)=x,f(x)=x2,...,f(x)=xrf(x)=1,f(x)=x,f(x)=x2,...,f(x)=xr

and it is not for f(x)=xr+1f(x)=xr+1

Simple Newton-Cotes formulas¶

They are interpolatory quadrature formulas where the nodes are equispaced within the interval. There are two types of Newton-Cotes formulas

  • Closed formulas. The integration limits aa and bb are nodes.

  • Open formulas. The integration limits aa and bb are not nodes.

Middle point rule¶

The Middle point rule is

∫baf(x)dx≈(b−a)f(a+b2)∫abf(x)dx≈(b−a)f(a+b2)

The formula can be generated substituting the function inside the integral by the zero degree polynomial that passes throught the point of the curve in the center of the [a,b][a,b] interval; that is, the horizontal line that passes throught the middle point of the function. Geometrically, we are computing the area of a rectangle.

Trapezoidal rule¶

The simple Trapezoidal rule is

∫baf(x)dx≈b−a2(f(a)+f(b))∫abf(x)dx≈b−a2(f(a)+f(b))

The formula can be generated substituting the function inside the integral by the one degree polynomial that passes throught the points of the curve in the boundaries of the [a,b][a,b] interval; that is, the line that passes throught two boundary points of the function in the interval. Geometrically, we are computing the area of a trapezoid.

Simpson rule¶

La fórmula de Simpson simple es

∫baf(x)dx≈b−a6(f(a)+4f(a+b2)+f(b))∫abf(x)dx≈b−a6(f(a)+4f(a+b2)+f(b))

Usar la fórmula de Simpson para integrar una función en un intervalo, equivale a sustituir, dentro de la integral, la función a integrar por el polinomio de interpolación de grado dos, que pasa por los puntos de la función de los extremos y el punto medio del intervalo. Es decir, sustituimos la función por una parábola e integramos.

The formula can be generated substituting the function inside the integral by the two degree polynomial that passes throught three points of the curve in the boundaries and the middle point of the [a,b][a,b] interval; that is, the parabola that passes throught these three pointsin the interval. Geometrically, we are computing the area under the parabola.

Composite Newton-Cotes formulas¶

A way to decrease the error is to increase the number of nodes using the composite rules. They are obtained splitting the interval [a,b][a,b] in nn subintervals and using, for each of this subintervals, the simple quadrature rule.

If we divide the interval [a,b][a,b] into nn subintervals of equal length using the nodes x0,x1,...,xnx0,x1,...,xn,

the length of a subinterval is

h=b−anh=b−an

the nodes are

xi=a+ihi=0,1,…,nxi=a+ihi=0,1,…,n

and the middle point of a subinterval is

x¯i=xi−1+xi2x¯i=xi−1+xi2

then, the composite formulas can be written:

Composite Middle Point rule¶

∫bafdx≈h∑i=1nf(x¯i)∫abfdx≈h∑i=1nf(x¯i)

Gaussian quadrature formulas¶

In the quadrature formula

∫baf(x)dx≈ω0f(x0)+ω1f(x1)+⋯+ωnf(xn)∫abf(x)dx≈ω0f(x0)+ω1f(x1)+⋯+ωnf(xn)

Is is possible to compute the weights ωiωi and the nodes xixi so the precision of the formula is maximum? Yes, but then, the nodos are not equispaced.

If we have nn nodes in [a,b]=[−1,1][a,b]=[−1,1] the weights and the nodes are

n12345wi2.0000001.0000000.5555560.8888890.3478550.6521450.2369270.4786290.568889xi0.000000±0.577350±0.7745970.000000±0.861136±0.339981±0.906180±0.5384690.000000nwixi12.0000000.00000021.000000±0.57735030.555556±0.7745970.8888890.00000040.347855±0.8611360.652145±0.33998150.236927±0.9061800.478629±0.5384690.5688890.000000

For instance, the gaussian formula with one node

∫1−1f(x)dx≈2f(0)∫−11f(x)dx≈2f(0)

that it is the same as the Middle Point rule. For two nodes

∫1−1f(x)dx≈f(−0.577350)+f(0.577350)∫−11f(x)dx≈f(−0.577350)+f(0.577350)

For three nodes

∫1−1f(x)dx≈0.555556f(−0.774597)+0.8888889f(0)+0.555556f(0.774597)∫−11f(x)dx≈0.555556f(−0.774597)+0.8888889f(0)+0.555556f(0.774597)

And so on.

This formulas can be generalized for any interval [a,b][a,b] changing xixi with yiyi following the

yi=b−a2xi+a+b2yi=b−a2xi+a+b2

And then, the quadrature formula is

∫baf(x)dx≈b−a2(ω0f(y0)+ω1f(y1)+⋯+ωnf(yn))∫abf(x)dx≈b−a2(ω0f(y0)+ω1f(y1)+⋯+ωnf(yn))
